Introduction

Soldering is one of those skills that's useful to have if you're interested in electronics. It lets you join electronic components together using molten metal.

Tools Required

Soldering does not need the most expensive equipment. A basic setup includes:
  • Soldering iron
  • Solder (many people say lead free is better)
  • Soldering iron stand
  • Flux (helps solder flow properly)
Optional but very useful tools:
  • Solder wick (removes excess solder)
  • Solder sucker (desoldering pump)
  • Helping hands clamp
  • Brass wool or sponge for cleaning the iron tip
Beginner kits are widely available and often inexpensive, making it easy to get started quickly. A good option if you would like to get started is this kit on Amazon, which retails for £13.69 (for my American friends, $18.03).

Safety

Safety is very important when soldering.
  • Always work in a well-ventilated area to avoid inhaling fumes. If you look on INstructables, you can find a few people have made fume extractor designs that you can 3d print and add a filter to.
  • The soldering iron tip gets extremely hot (around 300°C+)
  • Always return the iron to its stand when not in use
  • Wear safety glasses to protect from solder splashes
  • Wash hands after handling solder or electronics
  • Avoid eating or drinking near soldering areas
Soldering is safe when done carefully, but it is like anything, it can be dangerous if you make it dangerous.

Basic Steps

If you've never soldered before, don't overthink it. The process is pretty straightforward once you've done it a couple of times.

  1. Get your workspace set up and switch on your soldering iron.
  2. Wait for the iron to reach temperature.
  3. Clean the tip using brass wool or a damp sponge. A clean tip transfers heat much better.
  4. Place your component where you want it.
  5. Touch the iron to both the pad and the component lead so they heat up together.
  6. Feed solder into the joint, not directly onto the iron.
  7. Remove the iron and let the joint cool without moving it.
  8. Check your work. A good solder joint should look smooth, shiny, and securely attached.

Troubleshooting

Don't worry if your first few solder joints aren't perfect. Everyone runs into these problems when they're learning.

Some of the most common issues are:

  • Cold joints (they look dull and usually aren't very strong)
  • Not heating the joint enough
  • Dirty or oxidised pads and component leads
  • Using too much solder
  • Accidentally moving the component before the solder has cooled

Most of these are easy to fix:

  • Reheat the joint until the solder flows smoothly again.
  • Clean the surfaces before soldering.
  • Use a little flux if the solder isn't flowing properly.
  • Keep the component still until the solder has fully cooled.
